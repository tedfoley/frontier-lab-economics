Will Frontier AI Labs Make Money?

A capacity investment game under demand uncertainty: firms commit irreversible compute investment years before knowing if demand will materialize

Inspired by Dixit & Pindyck (1994) & Grenadier (2002)  |  Two-stage game: invest → discover demand → compete on capacity  |  Anthropic data for reference

Model Explanation — Formulas & Parameter Guide

Architecture

A two-stage capacity investment game (Dixit & Pindyck 1994; Grenadier 2002): firms commit irreversible compute spend years before demand is known, then compete on realized demand. All values are per-firm unless noted.

TAM & Capability Growth

g_cap = g_base + λ·((cumTraining_total / historical)^α − 1)
TAM(t) = TAM(t−1) × (1 + g_cap)

Cumulative training compute (including a historical base of ~$40B pre-2024 industry-wide) drives capability improvements via power-law scaling (Kaplan et al. 2020; α ≈ 0.07 = strongly diminishing returns). λ = scaling feedback strength. Unlike the log-ratio formulation, this depends on absolute cumulative compute — more training spend → higher g_cap.

Demand — Bass S-Curve with Novelty Dilution

noveltyFactor = 1 / (1 + ψ·max(TAM_growth_rate, 0))
r_d_eff = r_d · noveltyFactor
D(t) = TAM(t) × D_max / (1 + ((D_max−D₀)/D₀)·e^(−r_d_eff·t))

Adoption follows a Bass S-curve from D₀ toward D_max. Novelty dilution (ψ): when TAM grows fast, much of the market is newly addressable and hasn't started adopting yet, so effective diffusion slows. Overcapacity accelerates r_d via κ feedback (cheap prices drive adoption).

Open Source & Salvage Revenue

D_frontier = D × [(1−φ) + φ·(1 − q_os·σ_os)²]
demandLostToOS = D − D_frontier

Fraction φ of demand is contestable by open source. In overcapacity, firms don't let GPUs sit idle — excess capacity earns salvage revenue at a low margin (cheap API tiers, hosting open-weight models, fine-tuning services) from the demand pool OS captured.

Capacity & Hardware Depreciation

I(t): I₀ at t=0; then I(t) = I(t−1)·(1+g(t))
g(t) = capexGrowth·capexDecel^max(0, t−decelStart)
K_i(t) = θ · I(t−τ) · η(t) · (1−δ_hw)^age

Hardware depreciation (δ_hw ≈ 15%/yr): capacity depreciates net of algorithmic improvements. With τ=2 lead time, hardware has lost ~28% by arrival. Legacy capacity: when firms exit (entry/exit mode), their hardware enters a decay pool (30%/yr depreciation) rather than vanishing.

Revenue & Pricing

u = K_total / D_frontier (supply/demand ratio; u > 1 = overcapacity)
σ_eff = σ_base·(0.5 + 0.5·γ_eff), γ_eff = γ·(1−switchCost)
u ≥ 1 (overcapacity): Rev = (D_f/N)·(1/u)^σ_eff + salvage
u < 1 (scarcity): Rev = (D_f/N) · min((1/u)^ρ_eff, S_max_eff)
ρ_eff = ρ·(2 − γ_eff), S_max_eff = 1 + (S_max − 1)·(2 − γ_eff)

When supply exceeds demand (u≥1), prices collapse at rate σ_base amplified by competition, plus salvage revenue from excess capacity. When scarce (u<1), Scarcity Power ρ captures premium up to S_max, both modulated by γ_eff: differentiated products (low γ) amplify scarcity pricing; commoditized products (high γ) weaken it. Per Kreps & Scheinkman (1983), capacity constraints limit undercutting, but substitutability still modulates how much premium firms can extract.

Cost Structure

Cost_i(t) = I(t) + talentCost·(1+talentGrowth)^t + overhead

Costs = capex + growing talent cost + fixed overhead ($0.3B). Talent Cost grows at Talent Growth rate annually.
